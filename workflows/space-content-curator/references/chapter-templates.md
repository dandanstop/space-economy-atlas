# Space Content Curator Chapter Templates

Use this file when the site has five core atlas cards and each one needs a clear editorial identity. These templates are designed for `Space Economy Atlas`, but they also work for similar educational space-industry experiences.

## Shared Structure

Each chapter should usually contain:

- `summary`
- `why_it_matters`
- `how_it_works`
- optional `government_programs`
- optional `research_notes`
- optional `companies_to_explore`
- optional `future_signals`
- `sources`

Use progressive disclosure:

- first layer: 2 to 4 sentences
- second layer: short expandable sections
- third layer: source links and technical detail

## 1. Launch Sites

### Editorial goal

Help readers understand that launch sites are not just scenic backdrops. They are tightly engineered interfaces between geography, regulation, weather, logistics, range safety, and mission cadence.

### Summary should cover

- launch sites as critical infrastructure
- why location matters
- why ground systems shape launch reliability

### Why it matters

Focus on:

- sovereign access to space
- launch cadence and responsiveness
- economic clustering around pads, propellant, logistics, and testing
- safety and regulatory coordination

### How it works

Focus on:

- launch pads
- integration buildings
- fueling systems
- range systems
- weather and tracking
- countdown and scrub logic

### Good source mix

- NASA or agency explainer
- launch range operator pages
- university or lab material on propulsion operations, ground systems, or launch safety
- selected launch company infrastructure pages

### Best-fit extras

- `government_programs`
- `sources`

## 2. Rockets

### Editorial goal

Explain rockets as transport systems that convert stored energy into precise orbital delivery, while also showing why reusability, reliability, and manufacturing matter.

### Summary should cover

- rockets move payloads from Earth to orbit
- launch vehicle design shapes cost, cadence, and mission flexibility
- the industry is moving from one-off launches toward repeatable transport

### Why it matters

Focus on:

- access to orbit
- lower launch cost
- reusable systems
- national capability and commercial competition

### How it works

Focus on:

- stages
- engines
- propellants
- guidance and control
- payload fairings
- recovery and refurbishment

### Good source mix

- agency spacecraft and rockets explainers
- company launch system pages
- university propulsion or flight-dynamics labs
- papers or centers explaining reusability and performance tradeoffs

### Best-fit extras

- `research_notes`
- `companies_to_explore`

## 3. Satellites

### Editorial goal

Show that satellites are service platforms in orbit, not just hardware. Their importance comes from what they enable on Earth and across space systems.

### Summary should cover

- satellites sense, connect, position, and monitor
- different orbits serve different missions
- space hardware becomes useful when paired with ground and software systems

### Why it matters

Focus on:

- communications
- Earth observation
- weather
- navigation and timing
- resilience for civil and commercial systems

### How it works

Focus on:

- payload vs bus
- orbit selection
- power and thermal systems
- communications links
- tasking, data relay, and ground processing

### Good source mix

- NASA or ESA explainer pages
- remote sensing or mission pages
- university smallsat, imaging, or communications labs
- company sites for constellation or data-service examples

### Best-fit extras

- `government_programs`
- `research_notes`
- `companies_to_explore`

## 4. Space Stations

### Editorial goal

Present space stations as orbital laboratories and infrastructure nodes where research, habitation, robotics, maintenance, and future in-space industry converge.

### Summary should cover

- stations support long-duration operations in orbit
- they enable science, training, servicing, and system testing
- they may become stepping stones for commercial orbital activity

### Why it matters

Focus on:

- microgravity research
- human systems
- in-orbit assembly and servicing
- commercial LEO transition
- biomedical and materials research

### How it works

Focus on:

- modules
- docking
- life support
- robotics
- power and thermal management
- cargo and crew logistics

### Good source mix

- NASA commercial LEO and station pages
- JAXA, ESA, or other partner station research pages
- university labs working on microgravity, robotics, or orbital systems
- company pages tied to commercial station development

### Best-fit extras

- `research_notes`
- `future_signals`
- `companies_to_explore`

## 5. Industry

### Editorial goal

Turn a broad category into a readable map of the space economy. This chapter should connect upstream engineering to downstream services and highlight what readers usually miss.

### Summary should cover

- the space economy is a layered industrial system
- launch and hardware matter, but data, connectivity, compute, software, and services are equally important
- value is created across the full chain from manufacturing to downstream applications

### Why it matters

Focus on:

- national infrastructure
- climate, logistics, and communications impact
- spillover into telecom, mobility, AI, manufacturing, and defense
- long-term strategic resilience

### How it works

Focus on:

- upstream, midstream, downstream structure
- manufacturing and test
- launch and orbital operations
- data processing
- terminals, cloud, analytics, and applications

### Government programs

This chapter benefits from a dedicated government section. Aim for 3 or more meaningful links across major regions when the page can support it.

### Research notes

This chapter should usually include labs, universities, and mission-linked institutes because readers need help seeing where the next generation of capability is emerging.

### Companies to explore

Split when useful:

- public companies
- private companies

For each company, explain the role in the value chain.

### Future signals

Prioritize overlooked opportunities such as:

- on-orbit servicing
- orbital compute and GPU infrastructure
- autonomous operations
- in-space manufacturing
- debris tracking and removal
- dual-use sensing networks
- lunar logistics and cislunar infrastructure

### Good source mix

- WEF and agency ecosystem framing
- national programs
- university labs
- mission operators
- company official sites

## Writing Reminder

The five chapters should feel related, but not interchangeable.

- `Launch Sites` is infrastructure and location
- `Rockets` is transport and propulsion
- `Satellites` is orbital service delivery
- `Space Stations` is in-orbit habitation and experimentation
- `Industry` is the full value chain plus future opportunities
